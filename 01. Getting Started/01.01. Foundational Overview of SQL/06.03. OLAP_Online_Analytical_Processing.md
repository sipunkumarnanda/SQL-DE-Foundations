# 🤔 What is OLAP?

## 📖 Definition

**OLAP (Online Analytical Processing)** is a database workload designed for analyzing **large amounts of historical data** to support reporting, business intelligence, and decision-making.

Simply put:

> **OLAP helps businesses understand what has happened, why it happened, and what might happen next.**

Unlike OLTP,

OLAP is **not** responsible for daily transactions.

Instead,

it analyzes data collected from those transactions.

# ⚡ Characteristics of OLAP

OLAP systems have characteristics that are almost the opposite of OLTP.

---

## 1️⃣ Few Users

OLTP:

Millions of customers.

---

OLAP:

Usually used by:

* Managers
* Executives
* Data Analysts
* Business Intelligence Teams

The number of users is relatively small.

---

# 2️⃣ Very Large Queries

Suppose a manager asks:

```sql
SELECT
    department,
    AVG(salary)
FROM employees
GROUP BY department;
```

If the company has

20 million employees,

the database may need to scan millions of rows.

Unlike OLTP,

OLAP queries often read a very large portion of the database.

---

# 3️⃣ Mostly Read Operations

OLTP

Reads and writes happen continuously.

---

OLAP

Mostly reads.

Analysts rarely modify historical data.

They mainly:

* Read
* Aggregate
* Compare
* Analyze

---

# 4️⃣ Historical Data

OLTP focuses on

Today's transactions.

---

OLAP focuses on

Historical trends.

Example:

> Show revenue for the last 10 years.

---

# 5️⃣ Complex Calculations

OLAP performs operations like:

* SUM()
* AVG()
* COUNT()
* MIN()
* MAX()
* GROUP BY
* Window Functions
* Time-Series Analysis

These calculations may involve millions or billions of records.

---

# 🏗️ Internal Architecture of an OLAP System

A simplified architecture looks like this:

```text
Business Users

↓

BI Dashboard / Analytics Tool

↓

SQL Query

↓

OLAP Engine

↓

Data Warehouse

↓

Aggregated Results

↓

Charts / Reports / Dashboards
```

Unlike OLTP,

OLAP systems are optimized for reading and analyzing data rather than frequent updates.

---

# 📊 Example Query

Suppose management asks:

```sql
SELECT
    city,
    SUM(total_amount)
FROM Orders
GROUP BY city;
```

If the `Orders` table contains

100 million rows,

the database must:

* Read large amounts of data.
* Group rows by city.
* Calculate totals.
* Return summarized results.

This is a typical OLAP workload.

---

# 💾 Why Not Run This on OLTP?

Imagine customers are placing orders.

At the same time,

an analyst runs:

```sql
SELECT
    *
FROM Orders;
```

against

500 million rows.

The query consumes:

* CPU
* Memory
* Disk bandwidth

Customers may experience slower responses.

To avoid this,

companies often separate operational and analytical systems.

---

# 🏢 Data Warehouse

Most OLAP systems do not read directly from production databases.

Instead,

they use a **Data Warehouse**.

A Data Warehouse is a centralized repository that stores integrated historical data from multiple operational systems.

Example:

```text
Orders

↓

Payments

↓

Customers

↓

Inventory

↓

Data Warehouse

↓

Analytics
```

The warehouse is optimized for reporting and analysis.

# 🎤 FAANG Interview Questions

## ❓ What is OLAP?

### ✅ Answer

OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) is a database workload optimized for analyzing large volumes of historical data. It supports reporting, business intelligence, dashboards, forecasting, and strategic decision-making rather than day-to-day transactions.

---

## ❓ Why is OLAP mostly read-only?

### ✅ Answer

OLAP systems focus on analyzing historical data rather than modifying it. Analysts primarily perform read, aggregation, filtering, and reporting operations, while updates usually occur through scheduled data loading processes.

---

## ❓ Why don't companies run analytics on OLTP databases?

### ✅ Answer

Analytical queries often scan large datasets and consume significant resources. Running them on production OLTP databases can slow down customer-facing operations. To avoid this, organizations commonly separate operational and analytical workloads using data warehouses or dedicated analytics systems.

---

## ❓ Why does OLAP need historical data?

### ✅ Answer

Business analysis depends on identifying patterns and trends over time. Historical data allows organizations to compare performance across months, quarters, or years and make informed strategic decisions.

---

# 🎯 Key Takeaways

✅ OLAP is designed for analytical workloads rather than operational transactions.

✅ It processes large volumes of historical data.

✅ OLAP queries are usually complex, read-heavy, and involve aggregations.

✅ Data warehouses commonly power OLAP systems.

✅ Business Intelligence tools use OLAP data to generate reports and dashboards.

✅ Separating OLTP and OLAP workloads helps maintain both fast transactions and efficient analytics.
